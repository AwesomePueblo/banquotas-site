Introduction

The recent pro-Iran rally in New York City – held amid an escalating Israel-Iran confrontation – revealed deeply troubling undercurrents. What ostensibly began as a protest against U.S. or Israeli actions quickly became a “breeding ground” for open antisemitism, glorification of terrorism, and dangerous incitement to violence. A recent demonstration in Manhattan, documented by independent journalist Nate Friedman, made this terrifying reality all too clear. In a searing exposé – “I Exposed Iran ‘Protestors’” – published online, Friedman captured raw, unfiltered conversations with participants at the pro-Tehran regime rally amid the growing Israel-Iran conflic:contentReference[oaicite:33]{index=33}】. His on-the-ground interviews would reveal not only extreme rhetoric from the demonstrators, but also evidence that some of those in the crowd were organized, repeat agitators rather than ordinary grassroots protesters.

Friedman’s investigation builds on earlier efforts to expose such professional activists infiltrating protests. In this report, we summarize the key findings of his latest exposé: first, the extreme anti-Israel and pro-terror sentiments openly expressed by rally-goers; and second, the role of a coordinated “protest machine” in driving and amplifying the event. These findings raise serious questions about the true nature of these ostensibly spontaneous protests and who is really pulling the strings. Far from a simple gathering of concerned citizens, the New York rally appears to have been both a platform for radical ideology and a stage managed by seasoned operatives in the protest industry.

Antisemitic Rhetoric at the Protest

Friedman’s footage reveals that what was framed as an anti-war or “pro-Iran” rally quickly descended into unabashed calls for violence against Israel and praise for militant groups. In one jarring exchange caught on video, he asks a protester, “Do you want to see Israel destroyed?” – to which she immediately answers, “Yes”. She then doubles down, declaring that she is “thrilled” by Iran’s regime because it is “the only country that stands up to Israel and will hopefully destroy it”. This chilling admission set the tone for many conversations to follow.

Multiple demonstrators defended Iran and openly celebrated its terrorist proxies. Interviewees praised groups like Hamas and Hezbollah, with some even justifying Hamas’s infamous October 7th massacre of Israeli civilians as legitimate “resistance”. At one point, protesters chanted slogans such as “Iran make us proud, burn Tel Aviv to the ground”, explicitly endorsing the destruction of Israel’s largest city. The rally even featured members of Neturei Karta (a fringe ultra-Orthodox sect that opposes Israel’s existence) holding signs in support of Iran. Far from a peaceful anti-war demonstration, the event became an outlet for extremist rhetoric indistinguishable from the Iranian regime’s own propaganda.

Notably, protesters on American soil felt free to call for the literal annihilation of a U.S. ally. That individuals could stand in New York City and openly cheer the idea of Israel’s destruction – while excusing or even celebrating mass murder – is more than a shocking display of hatred. As one analysis warned, it represents a dangerous moral failure and a warning sign that should not be ignored. In short, the protest’s anti-Israel activism served as a thin veil for virulent antisemitism and the glorification of terrorism. This phenomenon underscores how quickly legitimate political discourse can be hijacked by those peddling violent bigotry under the guise of “resistance.”

Professional Protesters Exposed

Beyond the content of the protesters’ chants and slogans, Friedman’s investigation uncovered something equally insidious: the presence of professional agitators who travel from rally to rally. Partway through his interviews, a woman aggressively intervened – attempting to shout him down and prevent others from speaking. Suspicious of her behavior, Friedman dug into her identity. He discovered the woman’s name, Karen Shaw, and learned that she is a veteran activist who has been spotted at nearly 100 different protests over the years. In other words, she is not a random local outraged by this issue, but rather what Friedman calls a “professional protester.”

Shaw is affiliated with a New York City direct-action group called Rise and Resist, and she reportedly even admitted that working as a protest organizer is her job. Video from the event shows Shaw (wearing a neon protest marshal vest) attempting to corral the crowd and block Friedman’s camera, acting very much in an official capacity. Her extensive protest résumé – spanning causes from anti-police demonstrations to environmental and anti-Trump rallies – strongly suggests that her involvement in the Iran rally was part of a coordinated activist effort rather than spontaneous participation. In fact, Friedman identified several other familiar faces in the crowd, indicating that a network of repeat demonstrators was present.

Shaw’s case highlights the existence of an organized “protest industry” operating behind the scenes. These are people who make a career (or at least a regular practice) out of protests, traveling city to city to lend their voices and logistical support. Friedman has described it as a “paid protesters’ mob” – essentially a cadre of perennial activists who can be counted on to show up and agitate for various causes. The implication is that some large protests are in part manufactured: they are planned and bolstered by experienced organizers and paid staffers, rather than arising solely from grassroots energy. What might appear on TV as a spontaneous outpouring of citizen anger may actually be, to some degree, choreographed by professional protesters and the groups that deploy them.

Funding and Organization of Protests

The discovery of a professional protester like Karen Shaw naturally raises the question: who funds and coordinates these recurring demonstrations? In this case, Shaw’s organization, Rise and Resist, is a registered 501(c)(4) nonprofit that plays a key role in New York’s activist scene. Notably, Rise and Resist (like many similar left-wing groups) relies on the platform ActBlue to raise money. ActBlue is the Democratic Party’s premier online fundraising PAC and donation-processing system. For every dollar contributed through ActBlue, the platform pockets roughly four cents in fees – a seemingly small cut that quickly adds up, given ActBlue’s billions of dollars in donations. The platform is fueled by deep-pocketed progressive donors; ActBlue’s major benefactors include billionaire activists like George Soros, LinkedIn co-founder Reid Hoffman, and others.

How does this relate to street protests? It turns out ActBlue doesn’t just fund election campaigns – it also serves as a financial artery for many activist groups behind demonstrations. In early 2025, for example, Elon Musk pointed out that at least five left-wing activist organizations (including Rise & Resist, the Disruption Project, Indivisible, and DSA) responsible for orchestrating disruptive “Tesla protests” were fundraising through ActBlue. Reports confirm that these ActBlue-funded groups have been actively promoting and organizing such protests – some of which resulted in activists being arrested on charges like vandalism and disorderly conduct. ActBlue’s link to militant protest activity is nothing new, but much of it has gone underreported. The platform’s involvement in funding bail funds for rioters and facilitating pro-Palestinian campus occupations, for instance, has drawn criticism and scrutiny from lawmaker:contentReference[oaicite:48]{index=48}】. In short, ActBlue provides the financial infrastructure that can help turn local protests into sustained movements.

The coordination behind the “Solidarity with Iran” rally in New York was evident. The event did not materialize out of thin air; it was promoted by an alliance of socialist and pro-Palestinian groups (identified in one report as the “Bronx Anti-Iran coalition”) that called for supporters to hit the streets. On the ground, experienced organizers like Shaw were present to guide the protest. In fact, members of Rise and Resist had been preparing for that day as part of the broader “No Kings” nationwide demonstrations against President Trump’s policies. Rise and Resist volunteers served as marshals and safety coordinators during the massive anti-Trump marches in New York, receiving special training in de-escalation tactics to keep those protests orderly. That same organizational know-how was brought to the Iran rally. In other words, behind the scenes were seasoned activists, nonprofit resources, and a well-honed strategy – all working together to orchestrate what appeared to be a spontaneous outpouring of public sentiment. The result is a protest that, while genuine in the passion of many attendees, is also partly a scripted production enabled by political organizations and their donors.

Conclusion

The investigation into the New York Iran rally lays bare a two-fold truth: first, that extremist antisemitic ideology is increasingly masquerading as legitimate “protest” in the heart of Western cities, and second, that many of these protests are turbocharged by professional activism and organized funding. Far from an isolated incident, this rally was part of what Friedman and others describe as an organized global movement using popular causes as a Trojan horse for promoting hatred, justifying terror, and eroding historical truth. Protesters in Times Square freely called for Israel’s annihilation – a demand that is not merely shocking, but one that should ring alarm bells. It highlights a radicalization that we ignore at our peril. When chants of “burn Tel Aviv to the ground” can echo in New York, and when seasoned agitators can steer demonstrations from behind the scenes, it becomes clear that these are not normal times.

This case study underscores the need for greater awareness of both the messages and the messengers in contemporary protest movements. The blatant antisemitism on display should prompt leaders and communities to denounce hate unequivocally and ensure that legitimate political discourse is not hijacked by those inciting violence. At the same time, the revelation of a well-funded protest apparatus urges us to follow the money and organizational links behind such events. Only by understanding who is coordinating and bankrolling these rallies can we differentiate genuine grassroots outrage from manufactured theater. Friedman’s exposé provides a window into this hidden side of activism – a world where ideological fervor meets strategic planning and outside financing. In the end, shining a light on these dynamics is crucial: it helps inoculate the public against manipulation, and it affirms that while the right to protest must be protected, so too must our vigilance against those who would exploit that right to spread hate or chaos under a false flag of social justice.
